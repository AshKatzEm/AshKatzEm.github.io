Classroom data experiment

In the very beginning of 9/23, I was asked to teach high school algebra to students with learning disabilities through 6/24. I'm obsessed with trying to improve education. This was a silver platter offer to try out some of my ideas, many of which involved emerging educational technologies in one form or another. I designed a two year high school algebra 1 curriculum from the ground up and at the core, I used an educational platform called Quizizz. The goal was to minimize lecture as much as possible and instead learn through class-wide interactive problem solving. A massive ancillary benefit of this system was that it generated a steady stream of data. I documented my attempts to use it to inform future interactive lessons on LinkedIn. At the end of the day, I found that the data reinforced many of my preconceived teaching beliefs and made the challenges the students face more apparent. For example, the responses of students who "try" (subjective) can be predicted 85-95% of the time. So I'm able to predict how they will respond to a topic. If they are responding correctly, great, keep on teaching. If they are not responding correctly, we uncover the real challenge of trying to 'teach' them to respond differently. Finding the insights was easy, implementing them was difficult. Most of the work for this project was actually getting the data which program generated or should have generated. Quizizz has a nice dashboard but the problems start when you click the download button. I ended up learning a great deal about Excel, HTML, CSS, Selenium, Pyautgui, Plotly, Matplotlib, and feature importance in Scikit-learn. Overall, it was an incredible experience and it validated some of my ideas about teaching and opened my eyes to new ones. I think I learned more than the students, both in terms of teaching and technical skills, and they definitely learned some stuff. In this role, I used smaller class sizes, hands-on lectures, engaging technologies and classroom analytics to help learning challenged teens overcome their difficulties and intuitively understand Algebra. I designed a two year high school Algebra 1 curriculum from the ground up which relied on in-class, synchronous, interactive problem solving to both teach concepts through gradual, hands-on slides as well as give students who struggle with homework a chance to practice. Additionally, after every class, a downloadable data report composed of all manner of student performance data was produced. I continually loaded the data into a python notebook to visual student performance and gain insight into how to proceed in general as well as with particular students. I was able to correctly predict the response of a student to any particular question 85-95% of the time, depending on the student. By assigning all of the thousands of questions presented in class with numerous topical tags, I was able to accurately identify what elements of a new topic a particular student was struggling with. I also worked on detecting when a student was not taking an assignment seriously and excluding that performance data from the dataset. Overall, I was successful in finding deep insight and identifying areas of improvement from the data. The real challenge was implementing these ideas. While the data portal I had access to displayed good data, the associated data downloads turned out be lacking some of the most important data. Through learning an enormous amount about HTML, CSS, and Selenium, I build a bot which automatically downloaded a day's data, scraped the missing data from the portal pages and merged it with the day's data in a csv file. Most importantly, all of my students became more interested in math, many succeeded beyond their own expectations and some even started to feel that math was cool.
